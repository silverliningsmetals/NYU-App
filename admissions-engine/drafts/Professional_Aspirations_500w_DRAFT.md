## Draft v1 (~497 words, no em-dashes)

After Stern, I will join the strategy and operations practice of a firm like Deloitte S&O, Kearney, or EY-Parthenon, advising operations-heavy clients through the exact work I have already done in uniform: standing capability up, and winding it down, without breaking the mission in between.

I have lived both halves of that lifecycle. In the Pacific, I built supply operations where none existed, across five island nations with no infrastructure, rebuilding the plan every morning alongside the communities we served. In the Middle East, I ran the reverse, directing a 13.1 million dollar drawdown of 903 assets across seven bases without once interrupting operations, and brokering an agreement that cut transport-request time 81 percent. Building up and winding down is the full lifecycle clients bring to an operations consultant, and I have run it at coalition scale, on deadlines that did not move. I build outside the uniform too. I taught myself to code and launched Silverlinings Metals, a price-comparison platform I designed and wrote myself because a market inefficiency bothered me enough to fix it. The throughline of my career is one sentence: I see inefficiency, and I build the fix.

What I do not yet have is the formal analytical toolkit that turns an operator's instinct into a consultant's framework, and the cross-industry reps to apply it where I have never served. That gap is why Stern is the necessary next chapter, not a generic MBA.

Stern closes it in ways I cannot assemble anywhere else. The Supply Chain Management elective, taught by operations faculty like Professor Wenqiang Xiao, would let me convert the logistics I improvised in theater into the structured frameworks clients pay for. The Stern Consulting Corps would put me on a live client engagement for credit, so I practice the consultant's craft before recruiting begins, not after. The Management Consulting Association is the bridge from Marine logistician to first-year associate, with the case preparation and the alumni pipeline into the firms I am targeting. And Stern sits inside the city where Deloitte, Kearney, and EY-Parthenon staff and serve their clients, so the network I build in the classroom is the network I recruit into. Columbia shares the city, but only Stern pairs it with the program built for the rest of my story.

That part is the Fertitta Veterans Program and the Military Veterans Club: a cohort that has walked this exact transition, and a summer of statistics and accounting before the first day, so I arrive ready to compete instead of catching up.

If strategy and operations recruiting does not break my way, my plan B is internal corporate strategy or operations-transformation leadership at an industrials or defense-technology firm, applying the same operator-to-framework arc from inside the business.

Five years ago I bet that the Marine Corps would change me, and I won. Stern is where I place that bet on the next version of me, and this time I know exactly how to use it.
