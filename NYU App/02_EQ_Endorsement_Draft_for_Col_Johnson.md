### Question 1: Brief description of your interaction with the applicant and the applicant's role in your organization.

I served as Director of the J4 Directorate (Logistics) for Combined Special Operations Joint Task Force–Central, headquartered at Camp Xiphos, Jordan, from late 2024 through August 2025. 1stLt Ryan Degnan was my Logistics Current Operations Officer from 11 March 2025 to 9 August 2025, and during a separate ten-day period in late May 2025 he served as Officer in Charge of the Forward Supply Center at the Advanced Operations Base–Central in Iraq. CSOJTF-Central is a U.S.-led headquarters that coordinates special operations activities across roughly twenty countries in the Middle East, working with U.S. Marine Corps, U.S. Army, allied foreign militaries (including French, Iraqi, and Jordanian special operations forces), and the U.S. interagency. Ryan's role placed him at the center of a coalition of competing priorities, with daily responsibility for moving people, equipment, and information across that geography under both diplomatic and operational constraints.

### Question 2: How does the applicant's performance compare to other well-qualified individuals in similar roles? Principal strengths?

Over the past decade I have supervised more than forty junior officers in similar billets across multiple service branches. Ryan is in the top two of that group, and the only Marine I have given two end-of-tour decorations to in a single deployment.

His three principal strengths:

**Translating complex requirements into executable systems.** Ryan inherited a J4 that briefed logistics from spreadsheets stitched together each morning. Without being directed, he built the J4 MCS Common Operating Picture layer — a single real-time dashboard that ingested twenty-one countries' worth of cargo, personnel, diplomatic clearance, and vehicle-readiness data and surfaced it to senior leadership in a glance. Within a month it was the briefing tool I used with the Commander every morning. He also rebuilt the J4 SharePoint homepage and assembled a welcome-aboard packet for incoming staff because he saw — before anyone asked — that turnover was slowing the team down.

**Closing complex multi-stakeholder agreements.** As a First Lieutenant, Ryan facilitated a Key Leader Engagement that brought together U.S. Marine Special Operations leadership, the Iraqi Special Operations Forces Commander General Hadir, the Northern Line Haul transportation team, and our J4 staff. The agreement he helped negotiate reduced our cross-country shipping authorization times from 21 days to 4 — an 81% improvement that fundamentally changed how our task force could move. Officers ten years his senior could not have closed that room.

**Owning hard, unglamorous work.** When his team discovered an unaccounted-for $592,000 cache of equipment at the Advanced Operations Base, he could have signed it off and moved on. Instead he personally led the property-accountability process, returned 34 specialized tracking devices to their rightful unit, and rebuilt the property book. This is the work that quietly enables units to deploy safely. It rarely gets credit. He sought it out.

### Question 3: Describe the most important piece of constructive feedback you have given the applicant. Circumstances and the applicant's response.

About two months into the tour, I pulled Ryan aside after a senior-leader meeting in which I had watched him drive too quickly toward a solution before another principal — a partner from a coalition force — had finished framing the problem. The recommendation he made was correct on the merits, but the partner clearly felt unheard, and we walked out of the room with technical agreement and no real buy-in. He converted a piece of feedback into a behavior that produced one of the most consequential outcomes of our deployment. That is a rare adjustment to see in a junior officer in real time.

### Question 4: IQ+EQ example — emotional intelligence in a specific compelling example.

The most telling story I can offer is the Key Leader Engagement that ultimately produced our 81% reduction in cross-country shipping times. The technical accomplishment is on his award citation. The emotional intelligence behind it is not.

The meeting brought together five senior parties whose interests did not naturally align: U.S. Marine Special Operations leadership, who wanted faster movement of their teams; the Iraqi Special Operations Forces Commander General Hadir, who held sovereign control of the ground routes; the U.S. Northern Line Haul team, who carried the operational risk; our own J4 staff, who carried the bureaucratic risk; and our headquarters, who wanted a quick win. Ryan was the most junior officer in the room by at least two ranks.

He prepared for the meeting by spending the prior two weeks meeting one-on-one with each principal — not negotiating, just listening. He brought General Hadir tea twice. He asked the Northern Line Haul team to walk him through the route in person rather than on a map. He sat with the MSOC-IZ Liaison Officer through a logistics rehearsal he didn't need to attend. By the time the formal meeting happened, every party in the room had already told Ryan privately what they actually needed and what they were quietly afraid of. He arrived at the meeting having pre-sequenced an agreement that gave each party their most-needed concession while protecting their dignity in front of their peers.

When General Hadir raised an objection that surprised the U.S. side, Ryan did not defend the U.S. position or interpret. He paused, restated the General's concern back to him in his own words to confirm he had heard it, and then asked the senior U.S. officer present what flexibility we had. He created the space for the agreement rather than driving it. The resulting reduction in TMR processing time from 21 days to 4 was — in my view — produced not by a logistics innovation but by an emotional one. Ryan understood that a deal in that room would only hold if every party left feeling respected. He built that respect deliberately and patiently over weeks.

This is the EQ I have seen Ryan demonstrate consistently across the tour: a young leader who knows that the technical answer is the smallest part of the problem.

## Rating grid — guidance for Col Johnson

Stern asks the endorser to rate the applicant on a series of EQ-tied competencies. Critically, **a single "average" rating can sink the recommendation** at a top-15 program. Col Johnson should rate Ryan in the top tier on:

- Self-awareness
- Ability to receive constructive feedback
- Empathy
- Social/interpersonal skills
- Collaboration and team orientation
- Conflict resolution
- Communication
- Integrity / ethical judgment

If there is a category where he honestly believes Ryan is "above average but not exceptional," that's a credible signal — but Stern reads "average" as a quiet red flag. Real ratings, with the highest two being supported by the stories above.
