## A. Essay 1 — "Change: Embrace It" (349/350)

I made the Marine Corps a deal it never knew about: push me until I change or fail. I refused to come home the same person who left.

The Corps kept that bargain in ways I never saw coming. In the Pacific it handed me five island nations, no infrastructure, and a supply plan that rarely survived the morning, so we rebuilt it on the spot with the communities we came to serve, leaning on their knowledge. In the Middle East it sent me into a headquarters of soldiers, sailors, airmen, and coalition partners, the most junior officer in every room, every voice a different professional language. The roughest tasks never came with a manual. They came with a choice: sink, or learn to swim in water I had never seen.

I swam every time. Somewhere in those waters I found peace in being uncomfortable, and it became the key to my career. When something makes me feel like a beginner again, I walk toward it.

Now comes the biggest change of my life. I have accomplished what I set out to do in the Marine Corps, and I am leaving a steady career where I spent over half a decade building my craft. Calling that a big change is an understatement. I know exactly what the next version of me requires: the business acumen to match the leadership the Corps built. There is one way I know to manage change that size. Embrace it, fully.

That is my word, and Stern is where I will live it: two years as a beginner in New York, among classmates who refuse to coast, at the school that will ease this transition more than any other, beside a veterans cohort that has walked this exact road and will hand me the map, the way I intend to hand it to the officer who applies after me.

Five years ago I bet everything I had on the Marine Corps changing me. I won. Now I am placing the same bet on Stern, and this time I know exactly how to embrace it.
