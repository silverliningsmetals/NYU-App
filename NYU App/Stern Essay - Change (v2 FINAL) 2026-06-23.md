## Change: Embrace It

There is one way I know to manage change: embrace it, fully.

I wasn't always someone who walked toward hard things. Before the Corps I stayed in my shell, even letting a college lacrosse captaincy pass me by rather than chase it. So I joined the Marine Corps to be changed. I wanted to be pushed until I became the leader the Corps demands, or washed out. I knew the person who left wouldn't be the one who came home.

The Corps tested me immediately. A month out of training, it handed me a supply billet empty for months and a deployment to Micronesia and Papua New Guinea ninety days away, with no plan. The mission was partnership: embedding with island nations that each did things their own way. I arrived with a plan and watched it fall apart on contact. The change I had to embrace was letting it go and rebuilding with the communities we served, on their terms.

Two years later, the Middle East dropped me into a coalition headquarters where every voice spoke a different professional language. I was the most junior officer there. Nothing came with a manual; each task was a choice: sink, or learn to swim.

Twice, as a junior officer, I chose to swim. In that water I found peace in being uncomfortable; it became the engine of my career. Now, when something sounds challenging, I walk toward it.

The biggest change of my life is here. I've done what I set out to do, and I'm leaving a career I spent half a decade building. But the next version of me needs business acumen to match the leadership the Corps built.

Embrace is my verb, and Stern is where I'll keep living it—the one top program that picks its class for EQ as much as IQ, the same instinct the Corps built in me.

Five years ago I bet everything on the Corps changing me. That bet paid out more than I imagined. Now I'm placing the same bet on Stern, and this time I know exactly how to embrace it.

After Stern, I hope to join the strategy and operations practice of a firm like Deloitte, Kearney, or EY-Parthenon. Consulting drew me the first time I saw it described: high-impact work in ambiguous, team-based environments. I have been fortunate to live those conditions twice, building supply operations from nothing across five Pacific island nations, then responsibly drawing down networks across seven bases in the Middle East. Building up and winding down are the full lifecycle clients face, and I am grateful to have learned both early. I bring an operator's instincts, and I am excited for Stern to add the analytical frameworks, the client reps through its consulting community, and two years in the city where these firms and their clients work. I would be proud to spend my career helping organizations through the kind of change I have come to love.
