## 3. Stern's Evaluation Philosophy — IQ + EQ (in Stern's Own Framing)

Stern's central doctrine is **"IQ + EQ,"** treated as a **co-equal core value**, not a tiebreaker. This is directly on the record from Stern leadership, not consultant interpretation.

- **Official EQ Endorsement page:** "IQ+EQ is a core value of NYU Stern," seeking candidates with "both intellectual and interpersonal strengths." ([stern.nyu.edu EQ endorsement](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/eq-endorsement)) — high confidence.
- **Lindsay Loyd** (Clear Admit, June 19, 2025): "Our emphasis on both intellectual intelligence and emotional intelligence, what we call IQ + EQ, is top of mind throughout the reading and evaluation process," and "We strive to build a class and community of individuals who demonstrate IQ + EQ — this is core to our culture, and recruiters seek these qualities." ([Clear Admit Q&A](https://www.clearadmit.com/2025/06/admissions-director-qa-loyd-nyu-stern/)) — high.
- **Isser Gallogly** (P&Q, June 11, 2018): "IQ plus EQ is at our core." ([P&Q 2018](https://poetsandquants.com/2018/06/11/nyu-sterns-gatekeeper-on-the-new-eq-focused-application/)) — high.

**The three evaluation lenses** (Gallogly, [P&Q Aug 9, 2015](https://poetsandquants.com/2015/08/09/the-mba-gatekeeper-at-nyu-stern/), verbatim): the AdCom evaluates "how well applicants would do in the classroom; how successful they would be in their careers; and what kind of character they have," adding "The piece that people don't talk about as much is the character." **[VERIFIER]** the "Three Cs" label is the draft/consultant shorthand — Gallogly states the three dimensions but the source does not use the literal trademark phrase. Use "classroom / career / character," not a claimed "Three Cs" brand.

**The EQ Stern rewards — four competencies** (official EQ page, verbatim): **self-awareness, empathy, communication, and self-management.** **[VERIFIER]** Loyd's essay-context list says "self-awareness, empathy, communication, resilience, openness to new ideas and diverse perspectives" — slightly different wording ("resilience" rather than "self-management"). The four-competency official list is the canonical one.

**Self-awareness is rewarded, not penalized.** Gallogly (P&Q 2015, verbatim): "The biggest sign of strength is being OK saying what your weakness is. Self awareness is essential in the journey as a professional" (the Superman/Kryptonite analogy). Naming a genuine weakness is a strength signal — high confidence.

**Insight over creativity.** Gallogly (P&Q 2015, verbatim): on essays, "It's not about how creative it is, it's how insightful." — high.

**Genuine holistic review.** Loyd (June 2025, verbatim): "Every single application we receive is read in its entirety by multiple reviewers on the MBA Admissions team and evaluated holistically across three dimensions: academic potential, professional achievements and aspirations, and alignment with Stern core values." Reading is rolling/continuous, not batch-processed — Gallogly's timing advice: **"Apply as early as you are most competitive."** — high.

**Authenticity is a hard gate.** Official essays page (verbatim): "An offer of admission will be rescinded if you did not write your essays." **[VERIFIER]** extending this to AI-written essays is a **reasonable inference, not a quoted policy** — no on-the-record AdCom statement naming AI was found. Present it as inference.

**Leadership voices to cite:**
- **Isser Gallogly** — Associate Dean of MBA Admissions and Program Innovation; architect of the EQ-focused application (confirmed, Stern news, March 7, 2020).
- **Lindsay Loyd** — **[VERIFIER — title correction]** the draft used "Assistant Dean" (Clear Admit's term). Defer to **Stern's own title: "Executive Director for MBA Admissions"** (Stern news page, June 20, 2025). "Assistant Dean" is a single-source Clear Admit variant that conflicts with Stern's own page.

**Low-confidence / unverified:** the claim that "no other top school has a required EQ Endorsement" is **plausible but unverified** — no authoritative source exhaustively confirms uniqueness. State it as distinctive-but-unverified, not as fact.

## 4. Application Components — How Stern Reads Each (2026-27 cycle)

**Current essay set (2026-27 / Class of 2029)** — confirmed verbatim on the official essays page ([stern.nyu.edu essays](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/essays)):
1. **Essay 1 — Professional Aspirations** (500-word max, double-spaced, 12-pt)
2. **Essay 2 — Personal Expression ("Pick Six")**
3. **Essay 3 — Additional Information** (optional, 500-word max)

> **CYCLE CORRECTION — [VERIFIER], high confidence.** The **"Change: ___ it" essay (350 words) and the 150-word Professional Aspirations short answer were REMOVED for 2026-27.** They were a 2025-26 (Class of 2028) feature. The live page for 2026-27 shows NO "Change" essay and NO 150-word short answer. Any consultancy advice referencing a "Change essay" (Stacy Blackman tips, Stratus, Accepted, Fortuna's strategy article) is **prior-cycle** unless explicitly dated 2026-27. The original research draft's claim that the application has been "stable since the 2017-2018 redesign" is **wrong** and must not be repeated. Sources: official essays page; [Clear Admit 2026-27 analysis](https://www.clearadmit.com/mba-admissions-essay-topics-analyses/nyu-stern-essay-topic-analysis/); [Personal MBA Coach](https://www.personalmbacoach.com/nyu-stern-essays/).

### 4a. Professional Aspirations Essay (500 words)

**Exact prompt:** "What are your short-term career goals? Why is the Stern MBA the necessary next chapter in your professional story? Please be specific."

- **How Stern reads it:** the load-bearing written essay for goal clarity and program fit. The word **"necessary"** signals Stern wants why *this* program is indispensable — not whether an MBA generically helps ([Menlo, updated June 2026](https://menlocoaching.com/top-mba-programs/mba-essay/nyu-stern/)).
- **Wins:** a specific, credible short-term role/function/org type ("a strategy role at a growth-stage fintech," not "leadership"); named Stern courses, professors, clubs, experiential offerings that close a real skills gap; a "Why Stern" that **could not be pasted onto any other school**; alumni/event-informed specifics ([Clear Admit](https://www.clearadmit.com/mba-admissions-essay-topics-analyses/nyu-stern-essay-topic-analysis/)).
- **Sinks it:** generic "Why Stern" portable to any top-15/25 school — the single most-cited career-essay failure mode; vague goals; no specificity.
- **[VERIFIER]:** there is **no explicit "Why NYC" language** in the current prompt. The NYC emphasis comes from Loyd's commentary and consultancies, not the prompt text. Do not assert "Why NYC" as a prompt requirement.

### 4b. Pick Six (Essay 2 — Personal Expression)

**Structure (format-constrained, not word-constrained):** a single PDF containing a brief intro of **no more than 3 sentences**, **six images** illustrating "interests, values, motivations, perspective and/or personality," and **a one-sentence caption for each** of the six. Visuals may be photos, infographics, drawings, or any other images (official essays page — high).

- **How Stern reads it:** the AdCom's signature "fit" instrument. Gallogly (P&Q 2018, verbatim): "admissions meets Instagram" — it gives "a very quick view and impression of an individual — what they value, what's important to them." Treat it as a personality/values signal, not a writing-quality signal.
- **Captions carry the strategic weight.** Menlo: "The picture supplies the image; the caption supplies the why." Stern Admissions Director Nedjee Corriolan (via [Accepted](https://blog.accepted.com/nyu-stern-mba-essay-tips-deadlines/)): "the why means more than the what."
- **Wins:** a unifying theme; six **distinct** dimensions of the person; personal-over-professional balance (lead personal); captions that explain *why it matters*.
- **Sinks it:** a "second résumé" (diplomas, trophies, office shots); six images all conveying one trait; over-engineered/over-polished submissions (authenticity is the point); starting from "catchy slogans to wow the committee" (mbaMission — work backward from an authentic idea); **any format non-compliance** (wrong image count, missing captions, non-PDF) is a procedural red flag.
- **[VERIFIER]:** the "AdCom's favorite component" superlative is **consultant-sourced** (Stacy Blackman, Fortuna, Stratus), credible but not a direct AdCom quote — medium confidence on the superlative.
- The official page does **not** state a word limit for Pick Six; any "word limit" attributed to Pick Six would be invented.

### 4c. EQ Endorsement (recommendations)

Stern uses the EQ Endorsement **in place of traditional letters of recommendation** ([stern.nyu.edu EQ endorsement](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/eq-endorsement) — high):

- **One required, one optional** (a second is "optional, but not required").
- The required endorsement **must come from the current supervisor**; if not possible, an explanation must be provided in the application.
- A second may come from someone who knows the applicant **professionally and/or personally.**
- **Immediate family members may not** write an endorsement.
- Submitted online. No extra credit for an executive's title (per the Stern Full-time MBA admissions blog — "you won't get extra points for including a letter from an executive just because of their title").

**The four substantive endorser questions + optional fifth** (official EQ page, verbatim — high):
1. "Please provide a brief description of your interaction with the applicant and, if applicable, the applicant's role in your organization."
2. How the applicant's performance compares to **other well-qualified individuals in similar roles** — "Please provide specific examples. (E.g. what are the applicant's principal strengths?)"
3. "Describe the **most important piece of constructive feedback** you have given the applicant. Please detail the circumstances and the applicant's response."
4. (After framing IQ+EQ as a core value — self-awareness, empathy, communication, self-management) "...one specific and compelling example to demonstrate the applicant's **emotional intelligence.**"
5. "(Optional) Is there anything else we should know?"

**The rating grid** — **[VERIFIER — confidence flag].** The official Stern page only says endorsers "will be asked to rate you on several abilities and qualities" and **does NOT enumerate** the competencies or the scale. The 10-competency list and the 6-point scale are **Clear Admit-sourced, medium confidence** — verify against the live form if exact wording is load-bearing:
- **10 competencies** ([Clear Admit](https://www.clearadmit.com/mba-admissions-recommendation-questions/nyu-stern-mba-recommendation-questions/)): Analytical/Quantitative Ability, Initiative, Integrity, Leadership, Maturity, Professionalism, Oral Communication Skills, Written Communication Skills, Teamwork, and **Emotional Intelligence (EQ)**.
- **Scale:** Exceptional, Outstanding, Good, Average, Poor, Unable to judge.

**Why it matters to the reader.** Gallogly (Stern news, March 7, 2020, verbatim): "Some people don't like to brag... there is something about someone else giving the example that gives more resonance to it. It definitely gave us some insight on people in ways... I don't think would have gotten in any other way, other than bringing the person in for an interview." P&Q (2018): the endorsement "provides another dimension and lens for the candidate — especially in **borderline cases.**"

- **Wins:** an endorser who observed the applicant's EQ in action, briefed on the four EQ pillars and one specific moment (conflict resolution, difficult conversation, empathy under pressure), aligned with — not duplicative of — the rest of the file.
- **Sinks it:** a current-supervisor endorsement missing without a strong written explanation (a meaningful negative given the IQ+EQ emphasis); a title-chasing pick over a relationship-rich one; contradiction between the endorsement and the essays.

### 4d. Resume

- Read against a **comprehensive, non-blind interview** — interviewers probe resume specifics, so a thin or inconsistent resume narrative surfaces in interview and decision.
- **Wins:** quantified, civilian-legible impact; a coherent trajectory.
- **Sinks it:** jargon/acronym density; unexplained gaps (use the optional essay); inconsistency with stated goals.

### 4e. Optional Additional Information Essay (500 words)

Official prompt covers hardships, employment gaps, undergraduate-record/transcript explanation, and plans to retake the GMAT/GRE/EA/IELTS/TOEFL (official essays page — high; unchanged across recent cycles).

- **Wins:** genuine, forward-looking context (gaps, GPA/test concerns, reapplication framed as "transparency and forward-looking plans — not excuses," per Fortuna).
- **Sinks it:** using it as a fourth essay; duplicating existing content; excuse-making.

## 5. The FT MBA Test-Waiver Reality (FT vs. Part-Time, Resolved Definitively)

This is the single most error-prone area in the public discourse. **Resolved:**

- **There is NO automatic veteran/active-duty test waiver for the Full-Time MBA.** The automatic military/veteran waiver exists **only for the part-time Langone MBA**, where Stern states it "will be automatically applied to your profile." The Full-Time standardized-tests page lists **no** automatic military waiver. **[VERIFIER, high]:** confirmed by direct fetch of both pages. The FT page references the Fertitta Veterans Program by name but provides **no test-waiver provision** tied to it. Sources: [Langone tests-waivers](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/mba-programs/part-time-mba-manhattan/admissions/tests-waivers); [FT standardized-tests](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/standardized-tests).
- **The Full-Time (and Luxury & Retail) MBA is NOT test-optional.** "A valid standardized test score is required for all MBA applicants to the Full-time MBA and Luxury & Retail MBA programs." Accepted tests, no stated preference: GMAT, Executive Assessment, GRE, LSAT, MCAT, DAT. (The separate **Andre Koo Tech MBA is genuinely test-optional** — a distinct concept from a waiver. **[VERIFIER]:** the live page reads "The 2027 Andre Koo... MBA program is test optional," so the test-optional policy has carried into the current cycle rather than being frozen at "2025-26 only.")

**The only AUTOMATIC FT waiver:** NYU undergraduate alumni (including NYU Shanghai and NYU Abu Dhabi) with a final cumulative GPA of **3.20 or higher** are not required to submit a score for the Full-time 2-year MBA. **[VERIFIER refinement]:** this auto-waiver is **not FT-exclusive** — the official sentence extends it to the Full-time 2-year, Andre Koo Tech, Luxury & Retail, AND Langone Part-time MBA. (Note: "3.20 of higher" is Stern's own typo, reproduced verbatim.)

**Everyone else (including veterans) — by-request, discretionary waiver** ([FT standardized-tests](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/standardized-tests) — high): a separate online request **before applying**, requiring the request form, resume, all undergraduate/graduate transcripts, professional certifications (e.g., CFA, CPA), and a **300-word essay** — "Please list the coursework completed, certifications held, and professional experience you have that demonstrates academic readiness for an MBA program. You may use bullet points. (300 word limit)." Eligibility: bachelor's conferred before submitting; **one waiver request per cycle**; **dual-degree applicants ineligible**; **Consortium applicants eligible.** Reviewed rolling, decision "in most cases within 3 weeks." **Deadline: Thursday, April 1, 2027** (for the 2026-27 cycle). The committee weighs "an analytical or quantitative undergraduate or graduate degree, relevant professional experience, or certifications."

**Langone (part-time) categorical waivers — for contrast only, NOT available to FT applicants:** business major (GPA ≥3.20), engineering major (with committee approval), NYU undergrad (GPA ≥3.20), and active U.S. military/veterans.

### How a no-score / waiver file is treated

- **Officially neutral on admit/deny.** Stern and mbaMission agree applying for / being granted a waiver "will not have a negative effect on your chances." **[VERIFIER]:** the mbaMission wording is corroborated and **upgraded to high confidence** ([mbaMission](https://www.mbamission.com/blog/mba-programs-offering-gmat-gre-test-waivers/)).
- **But consultants strongly advise testing anyway** at a top-15. Menlo Coaching ([gmat-gre-test-waivers](https://menlocoaching.com/gmat/gmat-gre-test-waivers/), verbatim): "An applicant for a top full-time MBA should usually aim to submit their application with a test score — even if a waiver is possible," because "a strong test score is an advantage you can't afford to pass up." Crucially: "all other things being equal, admissions committees would rather admit an applicant without a test score than one with a below-average test score" (rankings pressure) — so a **low score is worse than no score**.
- **Stacy Blackman** ([best waiver candidate](https://www.stacyblackman.com/blog/whos-the-best-candidate-for-an-mba-test-waiver/)): the waiver itself doesn't hurt, but a poorly-executed waiver *request* does — "If you make it seem like you just want to avoid taking a test, it can hurt your application." Best waiver candidates have heavy quant backgrounds (financial modeling, calculus/stats/econ, CFA/CPA, engineering); "If you are early in your career, changing fields, or studied a more qualitative subject... not submitting a test score might raise doubts about your quantitative skills." SBC adds that **six of the seven M7 programs strictly require all applicants to submit test scores.**
- **mbaMission caveat:** for a weak academic record with thin quant coursework, "applying for a test waiver would not be strategic, as you are unlikely to have your waiver request approved."
- **Merit-aid cost.** Test scores are a primary merit-aid lever (Menlo: "many business schools still use test scores as a key metric for scholarship decisions"), so waiving likely forfeits the strongest scholarship signal **even when it does not cost an admit.** **[VERIFIER]:** specific numeric merit thresholds ("~720 classic / 675 Focus baseline; 740+ for large packages") trace **only to a third-party content site (test-ninjas.com), not Stern or a named consultancy — low confidence, illustrative only.**
- **Recruiting impact:** the claim that a waiver hurts IB/consulting recruiting is **unconfirmed** by any named-consultant source — do not assert it. The defensible point is the merit-aid impact.

**Reality check from the class:** for the Class of 2027, only **39% submitted a GMAT** and **~23% came in on a waiver**, so the waiver/GRE/EA path is a **substantial, legitimate route, not an exception** — but the record 737 average confirms score-submission is the norm. That ~66% submitted a GMAT or GRE; "most enrolled students submitted a competitive score" is a reasonable **inference** (Stern does not publish the exact waiver share).

## 6. The Veteran / Fertitta Lens

Stern is one of the most veteran-committed programs, anchored by the **Fertitta Veterans MBA Program**.

- **Founding:** established **2016** via a **$15M endowment** from Lorenzo Fertitta (MBA '93) and brother Frank J. Fertitta III; Stern describes it as believed to be the only program of its kind at a U.S. business school, for veteran/active-duty Full-Time MBA students (high; [Stern Fertitta page](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/academics/fertitta-veterans-program); [Clear Admit 2016](https://www.clearadmit.com/2016/11/generous-nyu-stern-alumni-gift-cover-half-tuition-veteran-active-duty-mba-students/)).
- **The money (current as of June 2026 — subject to annual change):**
  - **Fertitta Scholarship** — merit-based, reduces tuition to a **flat $30,000/year.** Admission is the gate; there is **no separate Fertitta application** (applicants check the merit-scholarship interest box). Critically, **not all admitted veterans receive it.**
  - **Yellow Ribbon** — Stern contributes up to **$33,100/year**, VA-matched for up to **$66,200/year** total (Chapter 33/Post-9/11).
  - **$20,000/year taxable living stipend** for Two-Year FT students on Chapter 33/Yellow Ribbon benefits who are **not** already receiving a merit scholarship (stipend and scholarship are mutually exclusive).
  - Application **fee waivers** for active-duty/veterans with service documentation (emailed to admchair@stern.nyu.edu).
- **Summer program:** ~**six weeks** (early July–mid-August), **two core courses / 6 credits** (Statistics & Data Analysis, Accounting), career programming, corporate visits, veteran-alumni mentorship, culminating in **LAUNCH** orientation. No separate application; preference indicated after the admission interview; scholarship/stipend eligibility is independent of summer-session participation.
- **Inaugural cohort:** 25 students (2016). **[VERIFIER]:** the "including a few non-scholars" detail is **not supported by a primary Stern source — treat as unverified.** The launch-era projection was **~20 scholars/year**, the only published scale figure. No fixed current annual count is published — treat current cohort size as not confirmable (low confidence).
- **Military Veterans Club (MVC):** student-run support/networking hub (career panels, veteran-leader speaker series, NYMEX charity fundraising, international-officer skills workshop). **Not an admissions decision-maker.**
- **Class share:** **9%** (Class of 2027), down from **11%** (Class of 2026). Use 9% as the current base rate and flag the downward trend.

### How Stern evaluates veteran applicants

- **Military background alone is not an edge.** Service to School: veteran applicants' test scores "closely align with the overall class averages," and "it's not the MOS itself that gives these candidates an edge — it's their academic performance and test scores." With the Class of 2027 GMAT at a record 737, **weight GMAT/GRE/GPA heavily for veterans**, not military service as a thumb on the scale. **[VERIFIER]:** this is general cross-school guidance — **no Stern-published veteran-vs-class academic breakdown exists.** Stern partners with **Service to School** (free veteran application counseling).
- **Translate the narrative.** S2S: "don't assume Adcoms will automatically understand the depth of your experience. Translate your stories into terms they'll understand."
- **The five documented failure modes** ([P&Q, Oct 23 2025, Emily Sawyer Kegerreis](https://poetsandquants.com/2025/10/23/5-common-mistakes-military-veterans-make-in-mba-applications/) — single-firm, client-base data, illustrative not Stern-audited): (1) copying a peer's "buddy" strategy; (2) leading with operational "readiness" instead of quantified business impact (rewrite "Maintained 95% operational readiness" as "reduced downtime by 40%... saving $200K annually"); (3) treating veterans' clubs as gatekeepers — "Veterans Clubs are NOT gatekeepers"; (4) defaulting to consulting (~80% of military clients start there, only ~30% end up there); (5) underestimating interview prep. Plus over-focus on military service with no broader community dimension (S2S).
- **How to stand out vs. the crowded logistics/operations-officer sub-pool:** quantified P&L/cost/efficiency results, a differentiated and credible non-default post-MBA path (supply chain, defense tech, corporate strategy, finance, entrepreneurship), dimensions beyond the job, and strong academics — **not** MOS or branch.

## 7. Interview Format, Reported Questions, Decision Timing & Waitlist

### Format

- **By invitation only**, **required for admission**, **comprehensive (NOT blind)** — the interviewer "will have thoroughly read your entire application," **conducted by trained admissions professionals** (occasionally another Stern administrator or a trained alumnus — FAQ-sourced, lower confidence on that sub-detail), currently **all virtual (Zoom)**. ([Notification process](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/notification-process) — high.)
- **Length:** ~**30 minutes** (current-cycle Clear Admit reports show 25 min in R3, 30–40 min in R4). The official page does not itself state a duration — the figure is Clear Admit/coaching-sourced, not official.
- **Invite rate:** Loyd (June 2025): "About 30 percent of applicants are invited to interview." Consultancies (Leland, Fortuna) estimate ~20–25%. **These disagree and only the ~30% figure is from Stern — confidence on the exact percentage is low; report a range.** An interview invite is itself a strong positive intermediate signal.
- **[VERIFIER — citation fix]:** the notification page lives at the **full-time** path (`/programs-admissions/full-time-mba/admissions/notification-process`), not the `/mba-programs/` hub.
- **[VERIFIER]:** pre-2020 Clear Admit "on-campus / role-play" reports are **stale format** — the current cycle is virtual.

### Most reliably reported question set

Three current-cycle Clear Admit reports converge (directional, small n — high on convergence, not statistically robust):
- A **"Pick Six"** image question
- **"Why MBA / Why Stern / Why now"**
- One or two **resume-walkthrough** questions
- One or two **behavioral / "tell me about a time"** prompts (often a problem-solving/challenge)
- **Short-term goal + a contingency / Plan B**
- **Internship / recruiting strategy**
- **Strengths & weaknesses**
- **Interests outside work**
- Then **~10 minutes for the candidate's own questions**

Sources: [R3 report](https://www.clearadmit.com/interview-reports/nyu-stern-mba-interview-questions-report-round-3-adcom-zoom-5/); [R3 report 6](https://www.clearadmit.com/interview-reports/nyu-stern-mba-interview-questions-report-round-3-adcom-zoom-6/); [R4 report](https://www.clearadmit.com/interview-reports/nyu-stern-mba-interview-questions-report-round-4-adcom-zoom/). The "Plan B / contingency" question is consistently asked — flag applicants whose post-MBA goal lacks a credible fallback. Poor judgment / inappropriate language in the interview is a real concern signal (Gallogly, P&Q 2015 — confirms profanity in interviews "happens more often than one might think").

### Decision timing

- At each deadline: one of three **initial** outcomes — **interview invitation, waitlist offer, or denial** — no later than the round's notification date. Candidates **may be waitlisted or denied without an interview.**
- **Post-interview decision: typically up to three weeks** (official, verbatim). Outcomes: offer, waitlist, or denial.
- Notifications begin shortly after each deadline and continue on a rolling basis.

### Waitlist behavior ([Waitlist FAQ PDF](https://apply.stern.nyu.edu/waitlistfaq.pdf) — high)

- **NOT ranked**; **no predetermined size** ("The waitlist is not ranked and the number of applicants on the waitlist varies... There is no pre-determined number"). Number admitted "varies from year to year."
- **No individual feedback** ("We do not provide specific feedback on individual applications").
- **Self-improvement levers:** retake — "We consider only your highest overall score(s), so there is no downside to retaking"; new credentials (CFA, CPA); updated resume; a **supplemental recommendation letter emailed by the author to admchair@stern.nyu.edu.**
- **[VERIFIER — corrections to the draft's waitlist claims]:** the live FAQ does **NOT** use the phrase "Waitlist Update Form" (it directs applicants to upload via the **application status page**, selecting "Supplemental Information"); its retake passage names only **"GMAT or GRE," NOT the EA**; and it does **NOT** name a "continued-interest essay" — instead it asks applicants to convey continued commitment and new involvement. The committee "reserves the right to make a final decision... prior to receiving additional material."
- **Campus visits don't help** — "The best way to demonstrate your interest is by uploading appropriate supplemental information via the application status page."
- **Timeline:** review begins in spring and "can continue until the orientation"; for the Two-Year FT MBA "a final decision may not be made until late August."
- **Real-world anecdotes (LiveWire / DecisionWire):** waitlist admits seen as late as mid-to-late May, some by phone, with wide stat variance (e.g., a GMAT 700/GPA 2.94 waitlist admit who declined for lack of scholarship; a GRE 316 waitlist admit; a GMAT 615/GPA 3.74 R4 denial; an EA 154 R2 waitlist-reject). **[VERIFIER — low confidence / unverified]:** these are **self-reported, transient, and could not be independently re-located.** Treat them as **illustrative only, never as benchmarks.** They skew toward late rounds (R3/R4) and waitlist cases.

## 9. Consolidated Catalog — RED FLAGS and GREEN FLAGS

### RED FLAGS / failure modes

**Process & authenticity**
- Ghostwritten or not-self-authored essays → **offer rescinded** (hard gate; AI extension is reasonable inference, not stated policy).
- Not reading instructions or email correspondence; cutting corners / "not doing the work"; complaining about clearly-spelled-out requirements (consultant synthesis of Stern guidance via Stacy Blackman — **medium confidence**, not a direct AdCom quote).
- Pick Six format non-compliance (wrong image count, missing captions, non-PDF).

**Stats**
- Sub-690 GMAT (10th ed.) / sub-3.36 GPA — below the 10th percentile of the class; needs offsets.
- A **low score with no context** (an explicit rejection driver); a below-average submitted score is treated as worse than no score at all.
- Conflating the two GMAT scales (682 Focus is not a drop from 737).

**Positioning (the heaviest movers — three of SBC's four top ding reasons live here)**
- Generic, portable "Why Stern" usable at any top-15 school — the most-cited career-essay failure.
- Vague career goals; no credible Plan B / contingency.
- Overrepresentation (consulting/banking/engineering — and finance is now the single largest, growing pool at 31%) without differentiation; the "one-note achiever / superhero" presentation.
- Lack of self-awareness; a Pick Six that reads as a second résumé or repeats one trait.
- Inauthentic / over-engineered material.

**Interview**
- Poor judgment / inappropriate language (confirmed AdCom concern).
- Surface-level answers that "don't show depth."

**Veteran-specific**
- Jargon/acronym overload; leading with "readiness" over quantified impact; defaulting to consulting; treating clubs as gatekeepers; exclusive military focus with no broader dimension.

### GREEN FLAGS / what differentiates admits

- **Authenticity** — Loyd: "Be authentic and be yourself."
- **Candid self-awareness** — naming a real weakness is "the biggest sign of strength" (Gallogly).
- **Insightful (not flashy) essays** — "not how creative it is, it's how insightful."
- **Hyper-specific, non-portable "Why Stern"** tied to named courses/professors/clubs/experiential offerings and Stern's NYC finance/consulting/tech pipelines.
- **Third-party EQ evidence** — a current-supervisor EQ Endorsement built on one specific, compelling EQ anecdote, aligned with the file.
- **A cohesive personal narrative** + demonstrated leadership, teamwork, communication, and depth in one's field.
- **A coherent, theme-driven Pick Six** with six distinct dimensions and "why-it-matters" captions.
- **Quantified, civilian-legible impact** (especially for veterans).
- **Strong, competitive academics** — GMAT inside 690–760 (10th ed.) / 645–725 (Focus), GPA inside 3.36–3.89; a strong **GRE (~164Q/163V)** is fully competitive and should not be penalized vs. GMAT.
- **A real, attainable short-term goal in Stern's proven pipelines**, with a credible contingency.
- **Veteran status with Fertitta fit** — an institutionally supported tailwind (but conditional, not an automatic admit or automatic scholarship; the FT MBA has no military test waiver).
- **Applying R1/R2** (better for both admit odds and scholarship); applying "as early as you are most competitive."
